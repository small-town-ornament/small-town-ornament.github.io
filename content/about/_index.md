I grew up in Evergreen, Alabama, where taste was not a thing you announced. It was what you kept clean, what you made last, what you noticed because nobody was coming to notice it for you.

My family did not hand me a finished life. They handed me standards, work, manners, weather sense, a suspicion of waste, and the kind of pride that can be either useful or dangerous depending on the day. I learned early that a woman could make a room feel better by moving through it with intention. I also learned that charm without substance is just noise with lipstick on.

Alabama gave me the first language. The scholarship gave me the next one. At the University of Alabama, I learned how to hold two versions of myself at once: the girl who knew the price of everything because money had always mattered, and the girl in Crimson Cabaret lipstick learning how posture can change the way a room answers you. Biochemistry taught me discipline. Dance team taught me presence. Both taught me that details are never small if they are the thing keeping the whole structure upright.

Atlanta is where those languages started arguing with each other in public.

This site is the record of that argument: clothes that have earned their keep, walks that make the city legible, restaurants that do not need a publicist, rooms and porches and balconies that teach a person how to belong somewhere without disappearing into it. It is not a lifestyle brand. It is not a performance of ease. It is a journal with good light, strong opinions, and enough specificity to be useful.

If it was not worn, eaten, walked through, sweated in, noticed, loved, resented, or felt, it does not belong here.

Small-Town Ornament is what happens when a farm girl keeps the weather sense and builds the rest herself.
