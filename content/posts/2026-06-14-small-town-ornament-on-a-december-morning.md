The air turned sharp overnight, that rare Atlanta December snap where humidity drops just enough to make your coat feel like an actual choice instead of theater. I caught it on the walk from the car to the corner market on Moreland, hands shoved in pockets, watching a string of plastic holly and tiny glass bulbs twist around a telephone pole like someone couldn’t help themselves. {{< post-image 0 >}}

Inside the market the coffee’s still the same burnt roast they’ve been serving since before the neighborhood turned over twice. I took it black with the last slice of sweet potato pie from yesterday and stood near the window watching two women in matching camel coats debate a wreath made of dried oranges and bay leaves. {{< post-image 1 >}}

One of them mentioned a tree-lighting in Inman Park tonight. The other said she’d rather stay home and hang the ornaments her mother mailed from Macon. I liked the way she said “ornaments,” like the word itself carried weight and needed gentle handling.

On the drive back the radio played the same three Christmas songs every station rotates and the beltline was thick with runners in bright tights. {{< post-image 2 >}} A guy on a bike balanced a paper bag from the French bakery against his handlebars; steam from whatever was inside fogged the plastic. Later, texting with Maya about dinner, I realized I’d already started mentally arranging the evening like an ornament on the week: the table at the new spot that does the vinegar-pepper collards, the corner booth where you can still hear each other, the walk afterward if the weather holds. The pie tasted exactly like the one I grew up with. The coffee was still terrible. Both felt necessary.
