This morning Atlanta feels like it's wearing last night's party dress with today's errands. The air is thick after that dawn rain, heavy enough to curl the edges of every magnolia leaf along the Beltline trail, but not so sticky that people abandon their scarves.



I watched a woman in camel cashmere and running shoes jog past three teenagers in matching vintage track jackets, all of them moving in the same direction without speaking.

Stopped at that little place on Highland that's trying to do both bakery and wine bar before noon. The pecan sticky buns are still too sweet for me, but the coffee pulls come out right if you ask for it half-oat, and the woman behind the counter remembers that.

Two tables over, a couple who clearly just finished an argument were pretending the pastries could fix it; neither of them touched the shared plate.

Later the light changed and the whole neighborhood smelled like wet brick and somebody else's gardenias. On the porch swing at the end of the block an older man was stringing paper lanterns for what looked like an evening gathering.



The humidity makes the paper go soft at the edges already. Atlanta entertaining never really waits for perfect conditions; it just moves the date two hours earlier or adds another string of lights.
