The triathlon idea started as a dare, which is usually how trouble gets invited in politely.

Not a dramatic dare. Nobody slammed a hand on a table. Nobody said I couldn't. It was worse than that: someone mentioned it like it was normal. Like signing up to swim, bike, and run in public was the kind of thing a person could just decide to do before lunch.

I laughed first. That's important.

{{< post-image 0 >}}

I have a complicated relationship with fitness because I spent too many years in rooms where bodies were both instruments and evidence. Dance teaches discipline, yes, but it also teaches you how quickly discipline can start wearing a costume. I know the difference between training and punishment. I also know I can blur that line if I'm not paying attention.

So I made rules before I made a plan.

No heroic language. No transformation story. No pretending a race will fix my character. If I'm doing this, I'm doing it because I want to find out what happens when my body has to learn three different kinds of patience.

> No heroic language. No transformation story.

Running is the familiar argument. I understand its moods. The first mile lies, the second negotiates, and somewhere after that my brain stops trying to host a committee meeting. Biking is more mechanical, which I resent and respect. The bike doesn't care how pretty your intentions are. It wants the gear, the road, the pressure in the tires, the little practical facts I sometimes try to charm my way around.

{{< post-image 1 >}}

Swimming is the rude one.

Swimming has no interest in my posture history, my strong legs, my ability to look composed while uncomfortable. The pool strips all that down to breath and timing. I get in thinking I'm an athletic person and thirty minutes later I'm bargaining with a tiled wall like it has legal authority.

That's probably why I need it.

The strange thing is how ordinary the training looks from the outside. A towel over a chair. A water bottle rolling under the passenger seat. Wet hair at the grocery store. A bike leaning against an apartment wall like a roommate with expensive needs. None of it looks like a becoming. Mostly it looks like laundry.

{{< post-image 2 >}}

But something is happening in the repetition. Not a glow-up. Please. I have survived enough internet language. Something smaller and more useful: I am learning how to keep a promise to myself when nobody is watching, and how to stop turning every promise into a performance.

Some mornings I want to quit for reasons that sound sophisticated but are really just laziness in better shoes. Some mornings the body shows up before the mind does. Some mornings the swim is ugly, the run is sticky, the bike makes a noise I do not understand, and I still count it because counting is part of telling the truth.

I don't know yet what kind of triathlete I'll be. Maybe stubborn. Maybe slow. Maybe better at the story than the split time.

For now, I'm the woman who laughed at the idea and then looked up a training plan.
