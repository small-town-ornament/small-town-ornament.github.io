The light this morning came in soft and heavy at the same time. Sunday heat in Atlanta always feels like it starts deciding things early—by ten it’s already pressing against the windows, the kind that makes you second-guess whether you really need to leave the house. I didn’t. Not right away. I stayed on the back porch in the old linen dress that’s gone soft from too many washes, feet up on the railing, watching the dogwoods hold their shade like they knew something I didn’t.

Eventually the pull won. I laced up and headed for the BeltLine anyway, earbuds in but no music, because the city was loud enough on its own. The stretch between Krog Street and the Eastside Trail was the usual Sunday mix—runners moving like they were trying to outrun the humidity, families with strollers moving slower because kids don’t care about pace, and the occasional someone walking a dog that clearly made the decisions for both of them. I stayed on the shaded side where the trees actually do something instead of just pretending. The light still found ways through though, little coins of sun on the asphalt and on the backs of my arms.

What I noticed most wasn’t the usual BeltLine spectacle. It was the pockets that still feel like somebody actually lives here. The little concrete patio behind that one apartment building where someone had strung up mismatched string lights even though it was broad daylight. The row of old brick warehouses that haven’t been sandblasted into Instagram perfection yet—graffiti still layered and alive, not curated. The woman sitting on her fire escape with a book and a glass of something sweating in her hand, not looking at her phone, just reading like the heat was background noise instead of the main event. Those details felt like the real Sunday. The rest is noise we’re all learning to tune out.

{{< post-image 1 >}}

I stopped at the tunnel under the highway, the one where the murals keep changing. Today someone had painted over last week’s work with a big, unapologetic red handprint that covered half the wall. It wasn’t pretty. It wasn’t meant to be. It was just there, loud and present, the way Atlanta never quite lets you forget it’s still becoming itself. I stood in the relative cool for a minute longer than I needed, letting the echo of footsteps and the low hum of traffic wash over me. Felt human in a way the polished parts of the trail never quite manage.

{{< post-image 2 >}}

On the way back I cut through that little neighborhood edge where the new builds haven’t swallowed everything yet. Porches with actual chairs instead of staging furniture. A guy pressure-washing his driveway like it was a religious act. The smell of someone’s grill drifting out between houses—charred edges, maybe onions—and it made my stomach answer before my head caught up. I thought about stopping for a late lunch but kept walking. The heat had its own rhythm today, and I wasn’t ready to break it.

{{< post-image 3 >}}

Later, back on the porch with iced tea and the dress sticking in all the usual places, I kept turning it over. What still feels lived-in here is rarely the shiny stuff. It’s the mismatched string lights and the pressure-washed driveways and the person on the fire escape who hasn’t checked her phone in an hour. It’s the red handprint that didn’t ask permission to exist. Atlanta in Sunday heat keeps showing me that the version worth staying for isn’t the one that photographs clean. It’s the one that sweats and keeps going anyway.
