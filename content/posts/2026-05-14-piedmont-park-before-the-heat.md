The trick is getting there before the city starts acting like itself.

At 6:42, Piedmont Park still belongs to the people who made a quiet bargain with the morning: runners, dog walkers, one woman stretching beside the path like she has never once been late to anything. The air had already gone soft around the edges, which is Atlanta's way of warning you that the day intends to be rude.

I wore the black shorts that do not ride up, the tank that has survived more laundry than it should, and the old Crimson Cabaret habit of checking posture in every reflective surface. Some things leave your body. Some things stay and become useful.
