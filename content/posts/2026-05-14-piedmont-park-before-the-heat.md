The trick is getting there before the city starts acting like itself.

At 6:42, Piedmont Park still belongs to the people who made a quiet bargain with the morning: runners, dog walkers, one woman stretching beside the path like she has never once been late to anything. The air had already gone soft around the edges, which is Atlanta's way of warning you that the day intends to be rude.

{{< post-image 0 >}}

I wore the black shorts that do not ride up, the tank that has survived more laundry than it should, and the old Crimson Cabaret habit of checking posture in every reflective surface. Some things leave your body. Some things stay and become useful.

That early, the park is not empty. It is better than empty. It is occupied by people who are not yet performing the day. Nobody has opened a laptop on the grass with a little coffee tableau beside it. Nobody is trying to turn a walk into evidence. The dogs are still damp around the muzzle, the runners are still negotiating with their hamstrings, and the skyline sits over the trees like a rumor that may or may not concern you.

I started near the Active Oval and took the path toward Lake Clara Meer, not fast enough to call it a run and not slow enough to call it strolling. This is my preferred speed for thinking. Walking is too polite. Running makes every thought sound urgent. The middle pace gives a person room to edit herself. By the first curve, I had already decided not to answer one email, to return a dress I had been pretending would become useful, and to stop buying peaches from the grocery store when the Piedmont Park Green Market is right there on Saturday mornings at the 12th Street entrance, acting superior for a reason.

{{< post-image 1 >}}

Atlanta heat changes the body before it changes the day. You feel it in the first small compromises: shorter stride, lower ponytail, fewer heroic ideas. By July, everyone in the city is either adjusting or lying. But in May, before the full lecture begins, Piedmont Park offers a kind of preview. The shade still works. The grass still has some green argument in it. The benches are not too hot. You can believe in moderation.

The park is also a mirror for the version of fitness I can actually live with. I spent enough years around dance rooms and performance bodies to know the difference between discipline and punishment, though I still have to remind myself. A good morning in the park does not fix a life. It does not cleanse, reset, optimize, or make a personality out of electrolytes. It just gives the body a vote. Mine voted for movement, water, and a second pass under the trees before the workday got loud.

Near the meadow, I watched a group of women doing lunges in a loose line. One of them laughed so hard she had to stop. The instructor laughed too, which made me trust her. That is the kind of fitness I believe in: serious enough to matter, unserious enough to keep you human. Across the path, a man in a Braves cap was walking with a tiny coffee and a very large dog, both of them committed to different agendas.

By the time I looped back, the sun had started to flatten the pretty parts. The park was becoming itself in public: brighter, busier, more available for photographs. I was sweaty in the ordinary way, not transformed, not glowing, just present. That felt like plenty.

{{< post-image 2 >}}

There are mornings when Atlanta asks you to fight it. This one let me pass through before the heat took roll call. I went home with damp hair, tired legs, and that brief, useful feeling that the day had not gotten ahead of me yet.
